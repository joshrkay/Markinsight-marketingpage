If you're running a Shopify store in 2024, you've probably noticed that your Meta Ads dashboard says one thing, your Google Ads dashboard says another, and Shopify's built-in reports say something else entirely.

This isn't a bug. It's attribution.

## What Is Attribution (And Why Is It Broken)?

Attribution is the process of assigning credit for a sale to the marketing touchpoints that influenced it.

Every ad platform has a built-in attribution window — a period of time after someone clicks or views an ad during which they get credit for any purchase. The problem: **every platform attributes to itself**, using its own rules, its own windows, and its own data.

This means:

- Meta claims a sale because someone saw a Facebook ad three days ago
- Google claims the same sale because someone clicked a Shopping ad yesterday
- TikTok claims it too because the person watched a TikTok ad last week

**You can't add these numbers together.** You're double (or triple) counting.

## The iOS 14 Problem

Apple's App Tracking Transparency (ATT) framework, rolled out with iOS 14.5 in 2021, broke pixel-based tracking for iOS users. If a customer uses an iPhone and hasn't opted into tracking, Meta can't follow them from ad to purchase.

The result: **Meta's reported ROAS is inflated** for most brands. Their models fill in the gaps with statistical modeling that tends to over-attribute.

This is why brands suddenly saw their Meta ROAS "drop" after iOS 14 — the numbers before were inflated. The real performance hadn't changed as much as it looked.

## First-Party Attribution: The Solution

The best way to get accurate attribution in 2024 is **first-party data** — data you collect directly through your own store.

When a customer buys from your Shopify store, Shopify knows:
- Exactly what they bought
- Exactly when they bought it
- What UTM parameters (ad tracking codes) were in their URL

By combining Shopify's first-party order data with UTM tracking from your ad campaigns, you can build attribution that doesn't rely on pixels, cookies, or what ad platforms self-report.

## How to Set Up UTM Tracking

Every ad you run should include UTM parameters in the destination URL. These are small tags appended to your link:

```
https://yourstore.com/products/item?utm_source=meta&utm_medium=paid&utm_campaign=prospecting-aug24
```

The key UTM parameters:
- `utm_source` — the platform (meta, google, tiktok)
- `utm_medium` — the channel type (paid, email, organic)
- `utm_campaign` — the specific campaign name
- `utm_content` — the specific ad or creative

When someone clicks this link and makes a purchase, Shopify records the UTM data with the order. That's your attribution signal.

## Attribution Windows: What They Mean

An **attribution window** is how long after an ad interaction a platform claims credit for a sale.

Common settings:
- **7-day click, 1-day view** (Meta default) — credit any purchase within 7 days of clicking or 1 day of viewing
- **30-day click** (Google default for some campaigns) — credit any purchase within 30 days of clicking
- **1-day click** (conservative) — only credit purchases within 24 hours

For most DTC brands selling products under $200, a **7-day click / 1-day view** window is reasonable. For higher-ticket items ($200+), you may want a longer window since the consideration period is longer.

## Multi-Touch Attribution Models

Single-touch models (last-click, first-click) give all credit to one interaction. Multi-touch models distribute credit across multiple touchpoints in the customer journey.

**Last-click**: 100% credit to the final ad clicked before purchase. Easy to understand, but ignores all the work earlier touchpoints did.

**First-click**: 100% credit to the first ad interaction. Good for measuring which campaigns are best at generating new awareness.

**Linear**: Equal credit to every touchpoint. Simple, and often more accurate than single-touch models.

**Time-decay**: More credit to touchpoints closer to the purchase. Useful if you believe recency matters most.

For most DTC brands, **linear attribution** on a 7-day window is a good starting point. It's fair to all channels and doesn't over-index on the last click.

## Reading Your Attribution Data

When you look at your attribution dashboard, you're looking at the **assigned credit** for each channel — not necessarily the exact causal impact.

A few things to keep in mind:

1. **Meta's self-reported ROAS will almost always be higher** than what you see in MarkInsight. That's okay — MarkInsight uses first-party data, not Meta's modeling.

2. **Google Shopping tends to have strong last-click numbers** because customers often search for your brand right before buying. This can make Google look more valuable than it is on a linear model.

3. **TikTok view-through numbers can be misleading**. If you see strong TikTok attribution, make sure it's click-based, not view-through, before scaling.

4. **Look at blended ROAS**, not channel-by-channel in isolation. The channels work together.

## What Good Attribution Looks Like

A healthy Shopify store doing $1M–$3M in revenue typically sees something like:

- 40–60% of attributed revenue from Meta (brand + prospecting)
- 20–30% from Google (mostly Shopping + brand search)
- 10–20% from TikTok (awareness/prospecting)
- 5–10% from other channels

If one channel is claiming 90%+ of revenue, something is misconfigured. If channels are claiming more revenue than Shopify actually recorded, you have double-counting.
